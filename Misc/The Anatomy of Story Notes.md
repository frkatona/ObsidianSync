# Chapter 1: Story

#### the conventional language of storytelling is problematic

>The first obstacle is the common terminology most writers use to think about story.  Terms like "rising action," "climax," "progressive complication," and "denouement," terms that go as far back as Aristotle, are so broad and theoretical as to be almost meaningless.

> Three-act structure, albeit a lot easier to understand than Aristotle, is hopelessly simplistic and just plain wrong

>> Idea that every story for the screen has three "acts": the first act is the beginning, the second is the middle, and the third is the end. The first act is about thirty pages long. The third act is also about thirty pages long. And the second act runs to around sixty pages. And this three-act story supposedly has two or three "plot points" (whatever those are). 
>> A mechanical view of story, like three-act theory, inevitably leads to episodic storytelling.

### Change and The Dramatic Code

1) change is fueled primarily by desire - What do they want?
2) characters walk on two legs: acting and learning - What do they do, what happens, and what does it mean to them
	1) pursuit of desire leads to struggle, and struggle leads to change
	2) present change or illustrate why change was resisted

Change represented differently in different storytelling forms:
- Myth: widest character arc, birth -> death; animal -> divine
- Play: focus on main character's moment of decision
- American Film: show the small change undergone by pursuing a small goal with great intensity
- Classic Short Story: the few events that lead to a character's single important insight
- etc

> The focal point is the moment of change, the impact, when a person breaks free of habits and weaknesses and ghosts from his past and transforms to a richer and fuller self. The dramatic code expresses the idea that human beings can become a better version of themselves, psychologically and morally. 

### Connecting elements to form a story

- Linear
	- single main character from beginning to end
	- e.g., "most hollywood films"
- Meandering
	- "the form of rivers, snakes, and the brain"
	- e.g., The Odyssey, Don Quixote, Adventures of Huckleberry Finn
- Spiral
	- circles inwards towards a center, like a cyclone or seashell
	- character keeps returning to a single event to explore it at progressively deeper levels
	- e.g., Vertigo, Memento
- Branching
	- system of paths extending from a few central points, like trees and river basins
	- tends to be used by stories that emphasize simultaneous action
	- e.g., Gulliver's travels, It's a Wonderful Life
- Explosive
	- multiple paths that extend simultaneously, like volcanoes and dandelions

# Premise

Opening line:
> Michael Crichton doesn't have the deep human characters of a Chekhov or the brilliant plots of a Dickens. He just happens to be the best premise writer in Hollywood. Take Jurassic Park, for example. Crichton's story might have come from this designing principle: "What if you took the two greatest heavyweights of evolution—dinosaurs and humans—and forced them to fight to the death in the same ring?" Now that's a story I want to see. 


recommends taking weeks before starting to write scenes to flesh out the premise to "see the big picture" and "figure out the story's shape and development"

>the premise stage is the most tentative of the entire writing process. You are putting out feelers in the dark, exploring possibilities to see what works and what doesn't, what forms an organic whole and what falls apart

### developing a premise
1) write something that may change your life
	1) get some data on who you are—get it outside of you and in front of you to study from a distance
	2) make a wish list and a premise list
	3) as you study the lists, patterns will emerge about what you love
2) look for what's possible
3) identify the story's challenges/problems
