### Eric - Pain, Pain, Worry and Woe

He likes to make his body a part of the show, from the way he rocks his whole torso back and forth when leaning into the microphone to his liberal use of unnecessarily long, dramatic slides across the neck.

### Essay on Ego in Performance

The most devastating quality to lack in an open mic performance is ego—or at least something that could be easily mistaken for ego.  It's important to sense that the performer is aching to share something with the audience: a story, a feeling, a philosophy, *something*.  Importantly, the performer should appear to believe that the audience *ought to* hear what they have to show; that the audience is *excited* to hear them.  It would be cruel either to keep them waiting longer than you have to or to rush and deprive them of savoring.  After all, they're here to see *you*.

In my least graceful moments, I wonder why a person would take a stage with any less prideful a headspace than that.  The last things I want to see from a performer are signals of contrition and hesitation.  And don't confuse that with an expectation that the performance will be cheerful or skillful.  By all means, cry on stage, crack your voice, and miss your chords, but I had better not get a sense that you're sorry about it, that you wish you hadn't done it.  Keep those cards close to the chest if you don't have the strength yet to throw them away.  The difference here is meaningful for anyone who brings a half-baked show: if you're *proud nonetheless*, then it was brave and exciting to share your progress with me.  But if you knew enough to worry, then why did you choose to waste my time?

As an adult, I'm not generally a subscriber to Ayn Rand's philosophy, but this is one case where I feel that a person has a responsibility to act selfishly.  And perhaps 'act' is a better word here than I initially considered, because a musical performance is no less a performance than a stage play, and it carries with it the same central irony: the appearance of authenticity.  You should be yourself, at least so long as that person is entertaining.  Otherwise, be someone else.  A compelling truth is less valuable than a convincing lie when reality interferes with immersion.
