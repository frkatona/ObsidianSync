| Phenomenon                                                          | How it could create surface texture                                                                                                                                              | Testable signature                                                                                       | Clean test                                                                                                                                                                                                                           |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Photothermal cure gradient**                                      | A moving hot zone cures/solidifies the surface before surrounding liquid PDMS levels out.                                                                                        | Texture tracks laser path and scan speed; higher power/slow scan increases relief.                       | Match the same thermal history with a hot plate or focused IR heater; compare FTIR cure conversion maps.                                                                                                                             |
| **Temperature-dependent viscosity / gel-front freezing**            | Heating lowers viscosity initially, causing flow, then rapidly increases viscosity as crosslinking proceeds. Flow can freeze mid-motion.                                         | Texture changes sharply with pre-cure age and pot life.                                                  | Repeat at 0, 30, 60, 120 min after mixing. Same laser settings should produce different flow arrest.                                                                                                                                 |
| **Thermocapillary / Marangoni flow at PDMS–water interface**        | Temperature gradients change interfacial tension and drive lateral surface flow away from or toward hot/cool regions, piling material into ridges or trenches.                   | Ridges asymmetric with scan direction; tracer particles move laterally during exposure.                  | Add passive tracer beads; record side/top view. Change water temperature or add small surfactant and see if flow/texture collapses. Marangoni-driven polymer patterning and laser-induced thermocapillary flows are well documented. |
| **Solutal Marangoni flow**                                          | Local composition changes—uncured oligomer enrichment, curing-agent segregation, dissolved gas, leachables—create interfacial-tension gradients independent of temperature.      | Texture persists even when thermal gradient is reduced; sensitive to mixing ratio, age, additives.       | Compare standard 10:1 PDMS, off-ratio PDMS, and extracted/degassed formulations.                                                                                                                                                     |
| **Buoyant convection in water**                                     | Hot water above the laser spot rises and circulates, applying shear to the liquid PDMS surface.                                                                                  | Texture depends on sample orientation, water depth, and whether the surface faces up/down.               | Cure upside-down, vertical, and under different water depths.                                                                                                                                                                        |
| **Forced flow from scanning beam**                                  | A moving heat source creates a moving thermal plume and shear stress, dragging soft PDMS.                                                                                        | Surface features lean or trail behind the scan direction.                                                | Scan same line left-to-right and right-to-left; alternating scan directions should reverse asymmetry.                                                                                                                                |
| **Water boiling / vapor bubble nucleation**                         | If the interfacial temperature reaches local boiling/superheat conditions, vapor bubbles displace liquid PDMS and imprint pits, channels, or rims.                               | Sudden threshold behavior with power; visible bubbles; crater-like pits/rims.                            | High-speed video; degassed vs nondegassed water; vary water pressure or water temperature. Local photothermal heating of non-degassed water can produce vapor-rich bubbles that evolve into air-rich bubbles.                        |
| **Dissolved gas exsolution**                                        | Heating lowers gas solubility and nucleates air bubbles without true boiling. Bubbles can become trapped as cure proceeds.                                                       | Bubbles appear at lower temperatures than boiling; greatly reduced by degassing.                         | Use degassed water and vacuum-degassed PDMS, then compare with air-saturated water.                                                                                                                                                  |
| **Cavitation collapse / microjets / shock waves**                   | Rapid bubble expansion/collapse near a soft interface can mechanically dent, eject, wrinkle, or tear the curing PDMS surface.                                                    | Localized pits, radial damage, satellite bubbles, sharp irregular features.                              | Hydrophone + high-speed imaging. Underwater laser processing literature emphasizes plasma confinement, cavitation bubble evolution, and water-layer thickness as major controls on texture/material removal.                         |
| **Bubble oscillation and acoustic streaming**                       | Stable or semi-stable microbubbles oscillate under pulsed heating, pumping fluid and particles around them.                                                                      | Circular/halo structures around bubble sites; periodic features related to pulse rate.                   | Change laser repetition rate/modulation frequency while holding average power fixed.                                                                                                                                                 |
| **Gas transport through PDMS**                                      | PDMS is highly gas permeable; trapped bubbles can grow, shrink, or migrate as gases diffuse through the polymer/water interface.                                                 | Bubbles evolve after laser exposure, not just during it.                                                 | Time-lapse microscopy for minutes to hours after cure; compare degassed and nondegassed PDMS. PDMS gas permeability is frequently exploited for bubble removal in microfluidics.                                                     |
| **Laser-induced bubbles inside liquid PDMS**                        | The laser can nucleate bubbles within uncured PDMS itself; bubbles may float, collapse, or leave membrane-like structures.                                                       | Hollow features embedded below surface; features not eliminated by degassing water alone.                | Cross-section microscopy/confocal imaging. Recent work directly reports laser-induced bubbles in liquid PDMS leaving membrane structures near the surface.                                                                           |
| **Capillary leveling failure**                                      | A liquid surface normally smooths by surface tension, but curing can freeze it before leveling completes.                                                                        | Smaller features disappear if cure is slowed or post-heated before gelation.                             | Laser perturb surface at low cure conversion, then wait before final oven cure.                                                                                                                                                      |
| **Wetting-ridge / elastocapillary deformation**                     | As PDMS transitions from liquid to gel, water–PDMS interfacial tension can mechanically pull on the soft surface and create ridges.                                              | Features scale with modulus at the moment of gelation.                                                   | Change cure state before submersion; compare liquid, partially gelled, and nearly cured PDMS. PDMS–water interfaces have large interfacial tension contrasts, and soft PDMS can form wetting ridges under interfacial stress.        |
| **Dewetting / partial wetting of PDMS under water**                 | Liquid PDMS may retract from certain regions or contaminants/substrates under water, making holes/islands/rims.                                                                  | Texture appears even without laser after immersion or after mild heating.                                | Non-laser underwater thermal control; compare clean glass, plasma-treated glass, silanized glass, and contaminated substrates.                                                                                                       |
| **PDMS oil cloaking / oligomer enrichment**                         | Low-molecular-weight PDMS species can migrate to interfaces and alter wetting, possibly smoothing or destabilizing the surface.                                                  | Surface chemistry differs from bulk; hydrophobic, oily residue or altered contact angle.                 | Rinse/extract uncured mixture or cured surface; compare contact angle and XPS/ATR-FTIR. PDMS oils at water interfaces can cloak droplets and modify apparent surface tension.                                                        |
| **Polymerization shrinkage / stress wrinkling**                     | Crosslinking reduces mobility and can introduce local stress; differential cure depth can wrinkle or buckle a thin gelled skin.                                                  | Wrinkles perpendicular/parallel to thermal/cure gradients; appear after cure rather than instantly.      | Stop exposure immediately after partial cure and image over time; cross-section for cured skin over liquid core.                                                                                                                     |
| **Thermal expansion mismatch**                                      | Hot cured/gelled PDMS expands, then contracts on cooling against cooler uncured PDMS or substrate.                                                                               | Cracks/wrinkles after cooling; stronger at high peak temperature.                                        | Heat-cure with same temperature ramp but no laser spatial gradient.                                                                                                                                                                  |
| **Carbon black redistribution / aggregation**                       | Absorbing particles may migrate by flow, thermophoresis, bubble sweeping, or aggregation, changing local absorption and amplifying roughness.                                    | Darker material accumulates in ridges/rims; later scans heat more strongly.                              | Map carbon distribution by optical density/Raman/SEM-EDS if applicable; compare different CB loadings and dispersion protocols.                                                                                                      |
| **Photothermal runaway / self-focusing of heat**                    | Slightly darker or thicker regions absorb more, cure faster, become hotter, and form positive feedback.                                                                          | Texture is noisy/speckled rather than smoothly following the beam.                                       | Use very low absorber loading, better dispersion, or a nonparticulate dye absorber; compare.                                                                                                                                         |
| **Ablation / pyrolysis / chemical degradation of PDMS**             | At sufficiently high temperature, PDMS can decompose, eject material, carbonize, or leave rough residues rather than merely cure.                                                | Brown/black residue, mass loss, sharp craters, altered FTIR/Raman chemistry, smell/fume.                 | Weigh before/after, ATR-FTIR/Raman, SEM. Compare air vs water vs inert atmosphere.                                                                                                                                                   |
| **Direct laser machining of already-cured or partially-cured PDMS** | If local dose crosses an ablation threshold, texture is subtractive rather than flow-based.                                                                                      | Clean grooves/craters with debris or recast rims; weak dependence on cure chemistry.                     | Expose fully cured PDMS under identical conditions. PDMS laser micromachining and channel formation have been demonstrated with suitable laser conditions.                                                                           |
| **Plasma formation at high fluence**                                | Pulsed lasers can form plasma at the target or in liquid, driving shock, cavitation, and ablation.                                                                               | Bright flash, acoustic shock, abrupt ablation threshold.                                                 | Photodiode/fast camera/hydrophone; reduce pulse energy while keeping average power similar.                                                                                                                                          |
| **Water-layer optical effects**                                     | The water layer can refract, defocus, cool, trap bubbles, and confine plasma/cavitation.                                                                                         | Feature size/depth changes with water thickness.                                                         | Repeat at 0.1, 0.5, 1, 2, 5, 10 mm water depth. Water depth is known to affect underwater laser ablation morphology.                                                                                                                 |
| **Substrate heat conduction / reflection**                          | Glass, metal, sapphire, or ceramic beneath the PDMS changes heat spreading and can reflect/refract the beam.                                                                     | Same PDMS/water/laser gives different texture on different substrates.                                   | Run on glass, aluminum, black anodized aluminum, sapphire, and thermally insulating polymer.                                                                                                                                         |
| **Pulse overlap / scan-path accumulation**                          | A scanned engraver creates overlapping thermal pulses. Dose depends on spot size, scan speed, pulse repetition, hatch spacing.                                                   | Periodic texture spacing related to pulse spacing or hatch spacing.                                      | Calculate pulse spacing = scan speed / repetition rate; vary speed and repetition independently.                                                                                                                                     |
| **Beam profile / focus / speckle**                                  | Gaussian, astigmatic, or multimode beams create uneven heat maps; out-of-focus beams broaden/soften texture.                                                                     | Texture mirrors beam ellipticity or hot spots.                                                           | Burn paper/thermal paper or image beam profile; vary z-focus.                                                                                                                                                                        |
| **Laser-induced periodic surface structures, LIPSS**                | Coherent, polarized irradiation can make subwavelength ripples on polymers. Probably less likely for a typical engraver unless fluence/pulse conditions fit.                     | Regular nanoscale ripples with orientation tied to polarization, often period near the laser wavelength. | Rotate polarization; inspect by SEM/AFM. LIPSS are established on polymer films under appropriate laser irradiation.                                                                                                                 |
| **Surface contamination / cure inhibition**                         | Amines, sulfur compounds, organotin residues, plasticizers, surfactants, or substrate contaminants inhibit cure locally, causing soft/wrinkled/uncured patches.                  | Tacky regions remain after bulk cure; texture follows contamination pattern, not beam.                   | Cure-inhibition control: place suspect materials in contact with PDMS without laser. Dow’s Sylgard sheet explicitly lists several classes of cure inhibitors and recommends compatibility tests.                                     |
| **Water/moisture effects on formulation**                           | Water may not be the main inhibitor for addition-cure PDMS, but moisture, interfacial impurities, or hydrolysis/condensation side chemistry can affect surface cure or adhesion. | Surface cure differs only when immersed during cure; humidity/water chemistry matters.                   | Compare DI water, salt water, surfactant water, degassed water, and dry nitrogen.                                                                                                                                                    |
| **Mechanical rupture of a gelled skin over liquid PDMS**            | A thin cured skin forms first; expanding bubbles or internal flow rupture it into folds, blisters, or channels.                                                                  | Thin membrane fragments, blister roofs, collapsed domes.                                                 | Cross-section immediately after exposure; partial-cure then mechanically poke/pressurize.                                                                                                                                            |
| **Rayleigh–Taylor / density instability**                           | Hot, lower-density PDMS/water configurations can become unstable under gravity, especially if a less dense phase lies below a denser one.                                        | Finger-like or cellular patterns sensitive to orientation.                                               | Cure sample face-up vs face-down.                                                                                                                                                                                                    |
| **Rayleigh–Plateau breakup of filaments/rims**                      | A liquid ridge formed by flow can break into bead-like droplets due to capillary instability before curing.                                                                      | Chains of regularly spaced beads along a laser-written ridge.                                            | Change viscosity/cure rate; bead spacing should scale with ridge diameter.                                                                                                                                                           |
| **Evaporation-driven concentration gradients**                      | Local evaporation at hot spots concentrates oligomers/fillers/impurities and changes interfacial tension/cure.                                                                   | Ring-like deposits around bubbles or hot zones.                                                          | Use sealed cell/pressurized water to suppress evaporation/boiling.                                                                                                                                                                   |
| **Thermal damage or modification of the water-facing surface only** | The surface may become chemically or topographically different from the bulk due to oxidation, hydrolysis, or enrichment of silica/filler.                                       | Contact angle, ATR-FTIR, XPS, or nanoindentation differs at laser track.                                 |                                                                                                                                                                                                                                      |
